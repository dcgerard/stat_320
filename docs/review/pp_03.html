  • 3.11
  • Solution

What is the probability that exactly one of two successive children will be female?

\[\begin{align*} P[(\bar{A} \cap B) \cup (A \cap \bar{B})] &= P(\bar{A} \cap B) + P(A \cap \bar{B}) \text{ (mutually exclusive events)}\\ &= P(\bar{A})P(B) + P(A)P(\bar{B}) \text{ (independent events)}\\ &= 0.5 \times 0.5 + 0.5 \times 0.5 \text{ (substituting in probabilities)}\\ &= 0.5. \end{align*}\]

Cardiovascular Disease 1

A survey was performed among people 65 years of age and older who underwent open-heart surgery. It was found that 30% of patients died within 90 days of the operation, whereas an additional 25% of those who survived 90 days died within 5 years after the operation.

  • 3.13
  • Hint
  • Solution

What is the probability that a patient undergoing open-heart surgery will die within 5 years?

Let \(A\) be died in first 90 days. Let \(B\) be died between 90 days and 5 years. We are given \[ P(A) = 0.3\\ P(B|\bar{A}) = 0.25 \] We are asked to calculate \(P(A\cup B)\)

Let \(A\) be died in first 90 days. Let \(B\) be died between 90 days and 5 years. We are given \[ P(A) = 0.3\\ P(B|\bar{A}) = 0.25 \] We are asked to calculate \(P(A\cup B)\)

Note that \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive events. This is because you can’t die twice (some eastern religions excepted). This means that \[ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) \] \(P(A)\) was given to us. Let’s get \(P(B)\) from the law of total probability. \[\begin{align*} P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|\bar{A})P(\bar{A})\\ &= 0 * 0.3 + 0.25 * (1 \times 0.3)\\ &= 0.25 \times (1 - 0.3) \end{align*}\] (\(P(B|A) = 0\) because, again, you cannot die if you already dead).

So, putting these together, we get \[ P(A \cup B) = 0.3 + 0.25 \times (1 - 0.3) = 0.475 \]

Pulmonary Disease

Pulmonary embolism is a relatively common condition that necessitates hospitalization and also often occurs in patients hospitalized for other reasons. An oxygen tension (arterial Po\(_{2}\)) < 90 mm Hg is one of the important criteria used in diagnosing this condition. Suppose that the sensitivity of this test is 95%, the specificity is 75%, and the estimated prevalence is 20% (i.e., a doctor estimates that a patient has a 20% chance of pulmonary embolism before performing the test).

  • 3.21
  • Hint
  • Solution

What is the predictive value positive of this test? What does it mean in words?

  • Sensitivity: \(P(T^+|D^+) = 0.95\)
  • Specificity: \(P(T^-|D^-) = 0.75\)
  • Prevalence: \(P(D^+) = 0.2\)

We want

  • \(PV^+ = P(D^+|T^+)\)
  • Sensitivity: \(P(T^+|D^+) = 0.95\)
  • Specificity: \(P(T^-|D^-) = 0.75\)
  • Prevalence: \(P(D^+) = 0.2\)

We want

  • \(PV^+ = P(D^+|T^+)\)

Using Bayes rule, we have \[ P(D^+|T^+) = \frac{P(T^+|D^+) P(D+^)}{P(T^+)} \] So we can use the law of total probability to get \[\begin{align*} P(T^+) &= P(T^+|D^+)P(D^+) + P(T^+|D^-)P(D^-)\\ &= P(T^+|D^+)P(D^+) + [1 - P(T^-|D^-)][1 - P(D^+)]\\ &= 0.95 \times 0.2 + (1 - 0.75)\times(1 - 0.2) \end{align*}\]

So we have \[ P(D^+|T^+) = \frac{0.95 \times 0.2}{0.95 \times 0.2 + (1 - 0.75)\times(1 - 0.2)} = 0.4872 \]

It means the chance of having Pulmonary embolism given that oxygen tension < 90 mm Hg is about 0.487.

  • 3.22
  • Solution

What is the predictive value negative of this test? What does it mean in words?

We need \(PV^- = P(D^-|T^-)\). Using Bayes rule we have \[\begin{align*} P(D^-|T^-) &= \frac{P(T^-|D^-)P(D^-)}{P(T^-)}\\ &= \frac{P(T^-|D^-)P(D^-)}{1 - P(T^+)}\\ &= \frac{0.75 \times (1 - 0.2)}{1 - [0.95 \times 0.2 + (1 - 0.75)\times(1 - 0.2)]}\\ &= 0.9836 \end{align*}\]

It means the chance of not having Pulmonary embolism given that oxygen tension <>90 mm Hg is about 0.9836

  • 3.25

Suppose that a company plans to build a lead smelter in a community and that the city council wishes to assess the health effects of the smelter. In particular, there is concern from previous literature that children living very close to the smelter will experience unusually high rates of lead poisoning in the first 3 years of life. The projected rates of lead poisoning over this time period are 50 per 100,000 for those children living within 2 km of the smelter, 20 per 100,000 for children living > 2 km but ≤ 5 km from the smelter, and 5 per 100,000 for children living > 5 km from the smelter. If 80% of the children live more than 5 km from the smelter, 15% live > 2 km but ≤ 5 km from the smelter, and the remainder live ≤ 2 km from the smelter, then what is the overall probability that a child from this community will get lead poisoning?

Let

  • \(A_1\): living within 2 km
  • \(A_2\): living > 2 km but ≤ 5 km
  • \(A_3\): living > 5 km
  • \(B\): Lead poisened.

Then we are given

  • \(P(B|A_1) = 50/100000\)
  • \(P(B|A_2) = 20/100000\)
  • \(P(B|A_3) = 5/100000\)
  • \(P(A_1) = 1 - (0.15 + 0.8) = 0.05\)
  • \(P(A_2) = 0.15\)
  • \(P(A_3) = 0.8\)

Let

  • \(A_1\): living within 2 km
  • \(A_2\): living > 2 km but ≤ 5 km
  • \(A_3\): living > 5 km
  • \(B\): Lead poisened.

Then we are given

  • \(P(B|A_1) = 50/100000\)
  • \(P(B|A_2) = 20/100000\)
  • \(P(B|A_3) = 5/100000\)
  • \(P(A_1) = 1 - (0.15 + 0.8) = 0.05\)
  • \(P(A_2) = 0.15\)
  • \(P(A_3) = 0.8\)

We want \(P(B)\), which we can get by the law of total probability \[\begin{align*} P(B) &= P(B|A_1)P(A_1) + P(B|A_2)P(A_2) + P(B|A_3)P(A_3)\\ &= 50/100000 \times 0.05 + 20/100000 \times 0.15 + 5/100000 \times 0.8\\ &= 0.000095 \end{align*}\]

Let

  • \(A\) be male.
  • \(B_1\) be 20–39
  • \(B_2\) be 40–54
  • \(B_3\) be 55+
  • \(C\) be Diabetes.

We are given - \(P(A) = 0.48\) and \(P(\bar{A}) = 0.52\) - \(P(B_1|A) = 0.4\), \(P(B_2|A) = 0.32\), and \(P(B_3|A) = 0.28\) - \(P(B_1|\bar{A}) = 0.44\), \(P(B_2|\bar{A}) = 0.37\), and \(P(B_3|\bar{A}) = 0.19\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_1) = 5/1000\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_2) = 23/1000\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_3) = 57/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_1) = 7/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_2) = 31/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_3) = 89/1000\)

We are asked to calculate \(P(C)\).

Let

  • \(A\) be male.
  • \(B_1\) be 20–39
  • \(B_2\) be 40–54
  • \(B_3\) be 55+
  • \(C\) be Diabetes.

We are given - \(P(A) = 0.48\) and \(P(\bar{A}) = 0.52\) - \(P(B_1|A) = 0.4\), \(P(B_2|A) = 0.32\), and \(P(B_3|A) = 0.28\) - \(P(B_1|\bar{A}) = 0.44\), \(P(B_2|\bar{A}) = 0.37\), and \(P(B_3|\bar{A}) = 0.19\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_1) = 5/1000\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_2) = 23/1000\) - \(P(C|A \cap B_3) = 57/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_1) = 7/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_2) = 31/1000\) - \(P(C|\bar{A} \cap B_3) = 89/1000\)

We are asked to calculate \(P(C)\).

\[\begin{align*} P(C) &= P(C|A\cap B_1)P(B_1|A)P(A) + P(C|A\cap B_2)P(B_2|A)P(A) + P(C|A\cap B_3)P(B_3|A)P(A) + \\ &P(C|\bar{A}\cap B_1)P(B_1|\bar{A})P(\bar{A}) + P(C|\bar{A}\cap B_2)P(B_2|\bar{A})P(\bar{A}) + P(C|\bar{A}\cap B_3)P(B_3|\bar{A})P(\bar{A}) \end{align*}\]

Plugging in values, we get: \[\begin{align*} P(C) &= \frac{5}{1000} \times 0.4 \times 0.48 + \frac{23}{1000} \times 0.32 \times 0.48 + \frac{57}{1000} \times 0.28 \times 0.48 +\\ &\frac{7}{1000} \times 0.44 \times 0.52 + \frac{31}{1000} \times 0.37 \times 0.52 + \frac{89}{1000} \times 0.19 \times 0.52 \end{align*}\]

This is obtained by Bayes theorem \[ P(A \cap B_1 | C) = \frac{P(C|A\cap B_1)P(A\cap B_1)}{P(C)} = \frac{P(C|A\cap B_1)P(B_1| A)P(A)}{P(C)}. \] The numerator values are given, the denominator was calculated in 3.26.
