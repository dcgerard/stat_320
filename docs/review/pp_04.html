Basic Discrete Probability

  • 4.1
  • Solution

Evaluate the number of ways of selecting 4 objects out of 10 if the order of selection matters. What term is used to denote this quantity?

The book’s notation is \(_{10}P_4\). This is the number of permutations of 10 things taken 4 times. This is \(10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 = 5040\).

  • 4.2
  • Solution

Evaluate the number of ways of selecting 4 objects out of 10 if the order of selection does not matter. What term is used to denote this quantity?

This is the number of combination sof 10 things taken 4 times. The book notation is \(_{10}C_4\), but everyone else uses \(\binom{10}{4}\). \[ \binom{10}{4} = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = 210 \] Checking in R, we get:

choose(10, 4)
[1] 210

What is the probability that exactly 4 people out of 50 aged 60–64 will die after receiving the flu vaccine if the probability that 1 person will die is .028?

Let \(X\) be the number that die out of 50. Then \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(50, 0.028)\). We calculate \(P(X = 4)\) via

dbinom(x = 4, size = 50, prob = 0.028)
[1] 0.03833

\(np\) = 50 \(\times\) 0.028 = 1.4.

Health-Services Administration

The in-hospital mortality rate for 16 clinical conditions at 981 hospitals was recently reported. It was reported that in-hospital mortality was 10.5% for coronary-bypass surgery and 5.0% for total hip replacement. Suppose an institution changes from an academic institution to a private for-profit institution. They find that after the change, of the first 20 patients receiving coronary-bypass surgery, 5 die, while of 20 patients receiving total hip replacement, 4 die.

  • 4.8
  • Solution

What is the probability that of 20 patients receiving coronary-bypass surgery, exactly 5 will die in-hospital, if this hospital is representative of the total pool of 981 hospitals?

Let \(X\) be the number among the coronary-bypass patients that die out of 20. Then \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(20, 0.105)\). We want \(P(X = 5)\)

dbinom(x = 5, size = 20, prob = 0.105)
[1] 0.03747

  • Solution

What is the probability of at least 5 deaths among the coronary-bypass patients?

We want \(P(X \geq 5) = 1 - P(X \leq 4)\)

1 - pbinom(q = 4, size = 20, prob = 0.105)
[1] 0.05162

Cardiovascular Disease 1

The rate of myocardial infarction (MI) in 50–59-year-old, disease-free women is approximately 2 per 1000 per year or 10 per 1000 over 5 years. Suppose that 3 MI’s are reported over 5 years among 1000 postmenopausal women initially disease free who have been taking postmenopausal hormones.

  • 4.15
  • Solution

Use the binomial distribution to see if this experience represents an unusually small number of events based on the overall rate.

Let \(X\) be the number of MI’s out of 1000 over the 5 years. The probability of MI over that time is 10/1000 = 0.01. Then we have that \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(1000, 0.01)\). We want \(P(X \leq 3)\), because this probability tells us if \(X\) is unusually small:

pbinom(q = 3, size = 1000, prob = 0.01)
[1] 0.01007

So it is unusually small. We would only expect to see this 1% of the time.

  • 4.16
  • Solution

Answer Problem 4.15 using the Poisson approximation to the binomial distribution.

\(X \approx \mathrm{Pois}(1000 \times 0.01) = \mathrm{Pois}(10)\). Calculation \(P(X\leq 3)\) we get:

ppois(q = 3, lambda = 10)
[1] 0.01034
  • 4.17
  • Solution

Compare your answers in Problems 4.15 and 4.16.

They are close.

Pediatrics

A hospital administrator wants to construct a special-care nursery for low-birthweight infants (≤ 2500 g) and wants to have some idea as to the number of beds she should allocate to the nursery. She is willing to assume that the recovery period of each baby is exactly 4 days and thus is interested in the expected number of premature births over the period.

  • 4.19
  • Solution

If the number of premature births in any 4-day period is binomially distributed with parameters n = 25 and p = .1, then find the probability of 0, 1, 2, …, 7 premature births over this period.

Let \(X\) be the number of premature births out of 25. Then \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(25, 0.1)\). We want \(P(X = 0), P(X = 1),\ldots,P(X = 7)\). We can do this in R via:

pvec <- dbinom(x = 0:7, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
pvec
[1] 0.071790 0.199416 0.265888 0.226497 0.138415 0.064594 0.023924 0.007215
  • 4.20
  • Solution

The administrator wishes to allocate \(x\) beds where the probability of having more than \(x\) premature births over a 4-day period is less than 5%. What is the smallest value of \(x\) that satisfies this criterion?

This is the same thing as finding the smallest value \(x\) such that \(P(X \leq x) > 0.95\). This is exactly the 0.95 quantile from the binomial distribution.

qbinom(p = 0.95, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 5

So, 5 beds.

We can confirm by seeing if \(P(X \geq 5)\) is less than 0.05 and \(P(X \geq 4)\) is greater than 0.05.

1 - pbinom(q = 5, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 0.0334
1 - pbinom(q = 4, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 0.09799
  • 4.21
  • Solution

Answer Problem 4.20 for 1%.

Using the same logic, we want the 0.99 quantile of a \(\mathrm{Binom}(25, 0.1)\) distribution

qbinom(p = 0.99, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 6

So, six beds. Verifying, we get

1 - pbinom(q = 6, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 0.009476
1 - pbinom(q = 5, size = 25, prob = 0.1)
[1] 0.0334

To document this increase, a questionnaire was mailed to 100,000 U.S. nurses in 1976 and 1978, asking about any current or previous tumors. Thirty new cases of malignant melanoma were found to have developed over the 2-year period among women with no previous cancers in 1976.

  • 4.22
  • Solution

If the annual incidence rate from previous cancer-registry data is 10 per 100,000, then what is the expected number of new cases over 2 years?

\[ 100000 \times \frac{10}{100000} \times 2 = 20 \]

  • 4.23
  • Solution

Are the preceding results consistent or inconsistent with the cancer-registry data? Specifically, what is the probability of observing at least 30 new cases over a 2-year period if the cancer-registry incidence rate is correct?

The exact distribution is a \(\mathrm{Binom}(100000, 20/100000)\). We want \(P(X \geq 30)\)

Pulmonary Disease

Each year approximately 4% of current smokers attempt to quit smoking, and 50% of those who try to quit are successful in the sense that they abstain from smoking for at least 1 year from the date they quit.

  • 4.35
  • Solution

What is the probability that a current smoker will quit for at least 1 year?

\(P(\mathrm{Quit}) = P(\mathrm{Quit}|\mathrm{Try})P(\mathrm{Try}) = 0.5 \times 0.04 = 0.02\).

  • 4.36
  • Solution

What is the probability that among 100 current smokers, at least 5 will quit smoking for at least 1 year?

Let \(X\) be the number that quit. Then \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(100, 0.02)\). We want \(P(X \geq 5)\)

1 - pbinom(q = 4, size = 100, prob = 0.02)
[1] 0.05083
  • 4.37
  • Solution

An educational program was conducted among smokers who attempt to quit to maximize the likelihood that such individuals would continue to abstain for the long term. Suppose that of 20 people who enter the program when they first stop smoking, 15 still abstain from smoking 1 year later. Can the program be considered successful?

Assuming that it wasn’t, \(X \sim \mathrm{Binom}(20, 0.5)\). We use 0.5 instead of 0.02 because these are folks who have decided they want to quit. We want \(P(X \geq 15)\)

1 - pbinom(q = 14, size = 20, prob = 0.5)
[1] 0.02069

So we are very sure that the program is successful, since if it weren’t we would only have a 2% probability of seeing as many or more successful quitters.

One useful summary measure of geographical clustering is the number of rooms with 2 or more affected occupants. If the binomial distribution holds, what is the expected number of rooms with 2 or more affected occupants over the entire nursing home?

There are 13 2-rooms, 4 3-rooms, and 15 4-rooms. The expected number of rooms with 2 or more affected folks is \[ 13 P(X \geq 2) + 4 P(Y \geq 2) + 15 P(Z \geq 2) \] where the random variables were defined in 4.47, 4.48, and 4.49. We calculate these probabilities as

Cancer 2

The incidence rate of malignant melanoma in women ages 35–59 is approximately 11 new cases per 100,000 women per year. A study is planned to follow 10,000 women with excessive exposure to sunlight.

  • 4.52
  • Solution

What is the expected number of cases among 10,000 women over 4 years? (Assume no excess risk due to sunlight exposure.)

\[ 10000 \text{ women} \times 4 \text{ years} \times \frac{11 \text{ cases}}{100000 \text{ women year}} = 4.4 \text{ cases} \]

  • 4.53
  • Solution

Suppose that 9 new cases are observed in this period. How unusual a finding is this?

Let \(X\) be the number of new cases over 4 years. The exact distribution is Binomial with size 10000 and probability 4 \(\times\) 11 / 100000 = 0.00044. We want \(P(X \geq 9)\)

1 - pbinom(q = 8, size = 10000, prob = 0.00044)
[1] 0.03577

We can approximate this with a \(\mathrm{Pois}(4.4)\)

1 - ppois(q = 8, lambda = 4.4)
[1] 0.0358

So, somewhat unusual.
